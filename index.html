Is the process by which an organism acquires exogenous DNA, and it can occur in two ways: naturally or artificially.Natural transformation describes the uptake and incorporation of naked DNA from the cell's natural environment. Artificial transformation includes a wide variety of methods for inducing the uptake of exogenous DNA and it's regularly used to introduce recombinant DNA into host bacteria, generally E. coli.
Cellular Competence
To be able to perform artificial transformation, a method to increase likelihood of DNA acquisition must be performed. The most common method involves use of divalent cations like calcium chloride (CaCl2) to increase the permeability of the bacterium's membrane, making them chemically competent.
Modified from:
Another artificial method of transformation is electroporation, in which cells are shocked with an electric current to create holes in the bacterial membrane. Following transformation, normal bacterial growth allows repair of the bacterial surface and posterior selection of recombinant cells by taking advantage of the newly acquired DNA’s characteristics such as antibiotic resistance.

Heat Shock Method
For chemically competent cells to assimilate the exogenous DNA from the extracellular space they need to be affected by a heat shock. The heat shock permeabilizes the cell membrane by destabilizing it with a rapid temperature change, opening the pores in the membrane so that the exogenous DNA can enter into the cell. This method consists of three stages as follows.EndFragment
